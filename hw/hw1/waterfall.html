The waterfall software development model is the original and most striaghtforward of all the different models covered in this website. Essentially, an entire project is developed by first defining the exact specifications of the software and then proceeding stage by stage from those original specifications without an opportunity for iteration or going back to previous stages in order to create a final product.










Different stages

There are typically six different stages to waterfall development:
  1. System and software requirements: in this stage, the exact specifications of the project are determined.
  2. Analysis: schema, models, and business rules are created.
  3. Design: the software architecture is determined.
  4. Coding: the development, proving, and integration of the software is performed.
  5. Testing: the software is systematically tested and debugged.
  6. Operations: the installation, migration, support, and maintenance of the complete system is carried out.

Criticism

One major problem with the waterfall model is that clients may not entirely know what their requirements are before they see an example of working software and thus may need redesign, redevelopment, and retesting not supported by model. Another problem is that this model prevents changing the design of new software, thus trapping a development team in a design that is rife with unforseen problems. For these and similar reasons, the waterfall model is largely shunned by most current major developers.
